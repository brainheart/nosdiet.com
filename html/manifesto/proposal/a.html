A Book Proposal for The No S Diet

The No Diet

Just three rules and one exception:

  • No Snacks
  • No Sweets
  • No Seconds

Except (sometimes) on days that start with 'S'

That's it.

Too simple for you? Simple is why it works.

Overview

Is there really room for anything new under the sun as far as diet books are concerned? Hasn't every possible gimmick already been tried -- several times -- without making the slightest dent in the relentless advance of obesity?

There's one thing left: simplicity.

The No S Diet is a diet plan so short and simple that it fits on the cover of a book -- or a book proposal. It also fits on refrigerator magnets, mugs, pens, plates, bumper stickers -- see the "promotion" section, below. No pseudo science, no miracle foods, no counting calories or carbs or fat, no disgusting recipes or draconian meal plans, just plain old common sense, charmingly and mnemonically codified. In brevity: a mantra, a haiku, a spell. But down to earth as a kansas wheat field. It's the one approach people still have ears to hear after all the snake oil they've been sold. And it works.

Let me guess. You're thinking either "that's absurd! I mean it's cute and all, but it could never really work" or "maybe it works, but how do you turn a 14 word mantra into a book that we can sell?" I'll address each point separately.

How could something this simple possibly work? Precisely because it's simple -- or rather, following the Einsteinian dictum, "as simple as possible, but not simpler." For all its folksy charm, there's a lot of serious, practical wisdom packed into those 14 words.

Commercial diets are too complicated for people with outside interests to follow ("eating = math"). Conventional wisdom rules about diet are too simplistic and vague to be useful ("only eat when you're hungry"). The No S Diet finds the happy medium between the two. It's simple and specific. You have just enough direction to do your job, but not so much that you resent its imposition. You have a solid framework, but the details are up to you. You have sufficient guidance and sufficient freedom. It's a system without too much system.

The No S diet solves the right problem. Pretty much any diet will lose you weight if you stick with it. The really difficult problem isn't how to lose the weight, but how to stick with the diet. According to the FDA, 95% of dieters fail to do this for more than a few months at a time. And is it any wonder? Popular diets are heavily optimized for rapid progress. They may be effective in an abstract, biochemical sense, but they don't take human psychology and motivation into account and are therefore extremely difficult and unpleasant to be on for any length of time. What do you do after you lose 9 pounds of bellyfat in 11 days? If maintenance gets any attention at all it's as an afterthought. But maintenance is more important than progress. Progress is intrinsically temporary; maintenance is what you'll be doing the rest of your life.

The No S Diet give maintenance its proper focus. It does this by treating wieght control as a mental problem, not a physical one. It focuses on incentives and enforcability, on leveraging limited reserves of willpower to build sustainable habits. The simple, natural behaviors it prescribes soon become automatic and permanent.

Even successful dieters from other systems will find the no s diet appealing. "What next?" isn't a hypothetical question for them, and chances are "more of the same" is not an appealing answer. The no s diet is a good place to start, but it's a great place to finish. It's the ultimate maintenance diet, the diet to end all diets.

The No S Diet is enjoyable. It isn't just an unpleasant grind towards a distant goal. Maybe you wouldn't do it solely for pleasure, but you can do it also for pleasure. And when the goal is distant and doubtful, or boring because already (temporarily) attained, that makes all the difference. Popular taboo/miracle food diets like low carb or low fat fail miserably in this regard. At the outset, it might seem worth it to trade pasta for unlimited steaks (or vice versa), but it gets old fast. Diets like weight watchers that require you to be a full time calorie (or carb, or fat) accountant are time consuming and joyless. With the nosdiet, no pleasure is denied, just delayed and contained. Served up on the platter of limited opportunity, pleasures become even more pleasurable than they were before.

Competition

There are basically two kinds of commercial diets: taboo foods diets (low fat, low carb) and substance accounting diets (weight watchers, points, etc). The situation is complicated a little by hybrids (counting taboo food units) and fluff. Horrified scientists occassionally publish sober descriptions of what the ideal human lab rat should eat, but these fail so miserably as prescriptions that they're not even worth considering.

Common sense is slowly creeping into the picture, but only in a very masked way and unaccompanied by simplicity. The Leptin diet, as desconstructed on the no s diet bulletin board, seems to boil down to almost the same thing as the No S Diet, but it's heavily obscured by pseudoscience and fluff. It also lacks the necessary safety valve and motivation of S days. It's encouraging to see confirmation some of the basic no-s principles, and that they sell despite a totally incompetant presentation.

The 3 hour diet is the diet of the moment as I write this, and it has a lot of advantages over the above mentioned taboo substance or accounting diets. Indeed, it resembles the no s diet in some ways. The blurb on the cover of the book, for example, "it's all about timing" could also be applied to the No S Diet (and was, in fact, on the nosdiet.com home page, until I saw they'd taken it). But in other more important ways it is profoundly dissimilar. One can poke holes at the 3 hour diet from a bunch of directions, but the key issue that cuts to its core is this: there's too much timing. It's like living on orange alert all day long for the rest of your life ("oh crap, is it time to eat? Better set the alarm clock!"). Its reliance on overclocking your metabolism by precisely timed mini meals is the antithesis of the no s diet. The no s diet works with your natural clock, not against it. Much better to do what thousands of generations of skinny people have done than the precise opposite. As with other diets, 3 hour might work biochemically, but it's doomed psychologically.

The Author

I is neither a doctor, a nurse, a dietician, a nutritionist, a personal trainer, nor any other kind of health professional. I AM NOT AN EXPERT. This is a tremendous advantage.

Diet is not a problem that requires specialized expertise. It is a general problem with an obvious solution. If I were encumbered by any form of expertise, this obviousnessness wouldn't be nearly as striking. A doctor cannot convincingly say "DUH."

I am a librarian by training and a computer programmer by accident. I noticed I was getting a little too heavy (borderline obese, actually, if you want to get technical) and came up with this common sense willpower trick. I lost 20 pounds in two months and kept them off without spending too much time suffering or even thinking about it. People I've explained it to have responded enthusiastically and so I thought I'd stick it on the web. The response has been overwhelming.

I've since lost an additional 20 pounds (so total progress has been 210 to 170), but to be fair, that may have something to do with the fact that I started doing some exercise (also a routine of my own devising -- we'll talk about this in a separate proposal, believe me, you'll want to). This seems to be my natural, healthy weight. People now describe me as "lean," which is sweet music to a lifelong "husky."

I've been on the diet since March 2002, with no yo-yoing. Unlike most diets, that get more and more insufferable as the gimmick ages, no-s gets progressively easier and becomes largely unconscious. At this point I'm about as tempted to break the rules as a devout Hindu is to eat a cow, or a Muslim a pig. It's a great feeling.
